Analyzing Algae Blooms using Drones

Ever since humans started farming and growing food, we have always looked for ways to increase crop yield. Therefore, large scale manufacturers use fertilizer, primarily nitrogen-based fertilizers using the Habor-Bosh process since its advent in 1909. A benefit of using fertilizer is it repels bugs and other creatures from eating the crops, thus, boosting crop yield. It also adds nutrients for plants to grow more quickly. The problem is that spraying fertilizer is only 10-30% effective, meaning that the plants only absorb 10-30% of the fertilizers. This leaves 60-90% of fertilizers in runoff polluting streams, rivers, lakes, etc. The gradual increase of nitrogen and phosphorus levels in bodies of water from fertilizer runoff promotes rapid growth of algae called algae blooms, a consequence of eutrophication. As the large amount of algae die, microorganisms rapidly consume the algae leading to the deoxygenation of water and death of animals within the bodies of water.

The goal of this project is to detect and analyze algae in water to improve long-term environmental analysis. By creating a more affordable and simpler alternative to current algae detection methods, clients can manage, detect, and track algae patterns within their own bodies of water. Our design will capture multispectral images of water by drone flight. Those images will then be analyzed through an ArcGIS Pro plugin we have programmed.
